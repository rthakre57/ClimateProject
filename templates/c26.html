Radiative-Convective Models (RCMs)

Radiative-Convective Models (RCMs) combine radiative transfer equations with convective adjustment to model the vertical temperature profile of the atmosphere. These models simulate the balance between:

  • Radiative transfer: absorption and emission of radiation,
  • Convection: vertical heat transport due to instability.

Radiative Transfer Equation

In a plane-parallel atmosphere, the upward \((I_\uparrow)\) and downward \((I_\downarrow)\) longwave radiative fluxes are governed by:

\( \frac{dI_\uparrow(z)}{dz} = -\kappa \rho(z) \left[ I_\uparrow(z) - \sigma T(z)^4 \right] \)
\( \frac{dI_\downarrow(z)}{dz} = \kappa \rho(z) \left[ I_\downarrow(z) - \sigma T(z)^4 \right] \)

where:

  • \( z \): altitude,
  • \( \kappa \): absorption coefficient,
  • \( \rho(z) \): atmospheric density,
  • \( T(z) \): temperature at height \( z \),
  • \( \sigma \): Stefan-Boltzmann constant.

Convective Adjustment

If the lapse rate exceeds the adiabatic lapse rate \( \Gamma_a \), convection occurs and temperature is adjusted to restore stability:

\( \Gamma = -\frac{dT}{dz} > \Gamma_a \Rightarrow \text{Adjust } T(z) \text{ until } \Gamma \leq \Gamma_a \)

For dry air, the adiabatic lapse rate is:

\( \Gamma_a = \frac{g}{c_p} \)

where:

  • \( g \): gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s2),
  • \( c_p \): specific heat of air at constant pressure.

Example: Two-Layer Radiative-Convective Model

Consider a two-layer atmosphere with:

  • Surface temperature: \( T_s \),
  • Atmospheric layer temperature: \( T_a \),
  • Emissivity \( \epsilon = 1 \),
  • No reflection (blackbody approximation).

The energy balance equations:

\( \sigma T_s^4 = \frac{1}{2} \sigma T_a^4 + S(1 - \alpha) \)
\( \sigma T_a^4 = \sigma T_s^4 \)

Solving these equations yields:

\( T_a = T_s, \quad T_s = \left( \frac{S(1 - \alpha)}{\sigma} \right)^{1/4} \)

which is similar to the result from EBMs, but now includes vertical structure and convection.
