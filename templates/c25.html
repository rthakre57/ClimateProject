Energy Balance Models (EBMs)

Energy Balance Models (EBMs) describe the Earth's climate system by balancing incoming solar radiation with outgoing terrestrial radiation. These models are often used to estimate the Earth's average temperature.

Basic One-Box EBM Equation

The general form of the one-dimensional EBM is:

\( C \frac{dT(t)}{dt} = S(1 - \alpha) - \epsilon \sigma T(t)^4 \)

where:

  • C: effective heat capacity of the Earth's surface (J/m²/K)
  • T(t): global mean surface temperature at time t (K)
  • S: incoming solar radiation or insolation (W/m²)
  • α: planetary albedo (dimensionless)
  • ε: emissivity of Earth (dimensionless)
  • σ: Stefan-Boltzmann constant (\(5.67 \times 10^{-8}\) W/m²/K⁴)

Steady-State Solution

Assuming steady-state conditions (\( \frac{dT}{dt} = 0 \)), the equilibrium temperature \( T_e \) can be found by setting the energy balance:

\( S(1 - \alpha) = \epsilon \sigma T_e^4 \)

Solving for \( T_e \):

\( T_e = \left( \frac{S(1 - \alpha)}{\epsilon \sigma} \right)^{1/4} \)

Example Calculation

Assume the following values:

  • S = 1361 W/m² (solar constant)
  • α = 0.3
  • ε = 1 (blackbody assumption)

Then,

\( T_e = \left( \frac{1361 \times (1 - 0.3)}{4 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8}} \right)^{1/4} \)

Note: The factor of 4 accounts for the spherical geometry (Earth intercepts area \( \pi R^2 \) but emits over \( 4\pi R^2 \)).

\( T_e \approx 255 \, \text{K} \)

This is close to the Earth's observed effective radiative temperature, highlighting the utility of EBMs as a first-order climate model.
